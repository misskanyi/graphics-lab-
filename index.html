What is Matatu Culture?

Matatus are privately owned minibuses that form the backbone of public transport in Kenya, carrying millions of passengers across Nairobi and beyond every single day. Far more than just transport, they are rolling art galleries — covered head-to-toe in elaborate graffiti, neon lights, massive sound systems, and larger-than-life personalities. Each matatu tells a story: of the driver, the tout (conductor), the passengers, and the streets they call home.

The culture peaked in the early 2000s and has never slowed down. Regulations have come and gone, but the spirit remains. A matatu is not just a vehicle — it is a statement.

Famous Matatus You Should Know

🚌
MOOD

Mood (Route 23)

One of Nairobi's most photographed matatus. Famous for its full-body anime artwork, LED underglow, and a sound system loud enough to rattle your teeth. Operates around Umoja and CBD.

Anime • Neon • Bass
🚌
SOURCE

Source (Route 33)

A legendary Eastlands matatu known for hip-hop and trap murals, decked out with portraits of rappers and Nairobi street art. A cultural icon on the Kayole–CBD stretch.

Hip-Hop • Street Art • East
🚌
OG RONGA

OG Ronga (Route 58)

A Githurai-CBD veteran. The OG Ronga is known for afrobeats vibes, bold orange and black paint, and a tout whose commentary rivals a radio presenter. Beloved by commuters for decades.

Afrobeats • Vintage • North
🚌
THE BEAST

The Beast (Route 46)

Infamous for its monster-movie horror artwork and bone-rattling subwoofers. The Beast runs the Kawangware–CBD route and is a favourite of students at nearby colleges.

Horror Art • Sub-Bass • West
